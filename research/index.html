Selected research projects

Josephson Junction Fabrication Process Development and Electromagnetic Cat Qubit Design

Prof. Oskar Painter, California Institute of Technology (March 2025 - Present)

I develop the fabrication process for high-quality Josephson junctions (JJs) based on the PICT method for improved device coherence. I also design and fabricate an electromagnetic implementation of a cat qubit, which is a promising candidate for fault-tolerant quantum computing due to its inherent error bias and potential for hardware-efficient quantum error correction. This device will serve as a first step toward realizing a hybrid cat qubit incorporating a mechanical quantum memory.

On-Chip Coupled Optical Parametric Oscillators (OPOs)

Prof. Alireza Marandi, California Institute of Technology (January 2024 - Present)

I lead the design and fabrication of on-chip coupled optical parametric oscillators (OPOs) to demonstrate spectral phase transitions and to serve as a foundation for developing an on-chip optical Ising machine. I characterized the fabricated devices and tested for quasi-phase matching (QPM) through optical parametric generation (OPG).

Still in progress...

Development of a Qubit Module for Realizing Networks of Superconducting Quantum Processors

Prof. Oskar Painter, California Institute of Technology (April 2024 - March 2025)

I designed, fabricated, and characterized a superconducting transmon qubit module for integration with a quantum optomechanical transducer to realize a ‘node’ in a quantum network capable of emitting optical photons, which are entangled with superconducting qubits inside the node. To ensure efficient capture of microwave photons emitted by the transducer, I optimized the circuit geometry, using Sonnet, based on a prototype module to achieve better photon absorption efficiency. I also implemented asymmetric superconducting quantum interference devices (SQUIDs) to improve quantum coherence of the qubits. This qubit module can be implemented in a future quantum network distributing entanglement across remote superconducting processors.

Realizing Photonic Integrated Circuits on Thin Film Lithium Niobate

Prof. Alireza Marandi, California Institute of Technology (January 2023 - December 2023)

In this project, I worked on integrated photonics using thin-film lithium niobate (TFLN), a promising platform for low-cost, energy-efficient communication, sensing, and computing. Thanks to its strong nonlinearity, TFLN is emerging as a leading candidate for photonic computing. However, building a full optical computer requires integrating both linear and nonlinear elements on the same chip—something that has not yet been fully achieved. My goal was to help bridge this gap by optimizing a key component: optical couplers. I designed and tested several types of couplers using commercial electromagnetic simulation tools, Lumerical, and verified the results through multiple simulation methods, including FDE, EME, and FDTD. I then fabricated the devices and characterized their performance to evaluate their robustness and fabrication sensitivity.
